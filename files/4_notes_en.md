## 1. Bonding in Carbon - The Covalent Bond

The reactivity of elements is driven by their tendency to attain a noble gas configuration. Carbon has an atomic number of $6$, with an electronic configuration of **$2, 4$**. It has $4$ valence electrons in its outermost shell.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why Carbon does not form Ionic Bonds?**
> * **Inability to form $C^{4-}$ Anion:** If carbon gains $4$ electrons, it would be extremely difficult for the nucleus with $6$ protons to hold onto $10$ electrons (i.e., $4$ extra electrons).
> * **Inability to form $C^{4+}$ Cation:** If carbon loses $4$ electrons, removing $4$ electrons would require a massive amount of energy, leaving a carbon cation with only $2$ electrons held by $6$ protons.

### 1.1 Covalent Bond
Carbon overcomes this problem by sharing its valence electrons with other atoms of carbon or other elements.
* **Definition:** A chemical bond formed by the sharing of an electron pair between two atoms is called a **Covalent Bond**.

> [!NOTE]
> **Physical Properties of Covalent Compounds:**
> * Covalent compounds have strong bonds within the molecules, but the **intermolecular forces** are very weak.
> * As a result, these compounds have **low melting and boiling points**.
> * Since electrons are shared and no charged particles (ions) are formed, covalent compounds are generally **poor conductors of electricity**.

#### Electron Dot Structures

1. **Hydrogen Molecule ($\text{H}_2$ - Single Bond):**
   ```text
   H (•) + (•) H   ➔   H : H   ➔   H - H (Single Bond)
   ```
2. **Oxygen Molecule ($\text{O}_2$ - Double Bond):**
   ```text
   O (:•) + (•:) O  ➔  O :: O  ➔  O = O (Double Bond)
   ```
3. **Nitrogen Molecule ($\text{N}_2$ - Triple Bond):**
   ```text
   N (::•) + (•::) N ➔  N ::: N  ➔  N ≡ N (Triple Bond)
   ```
4. **Water Molecule ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$):**
   ```text
         H
        /
       O - H
   ```
5. **Methane Molecule ($\text{CH}_4$):**
   ```text
         H
         •
     H • C • H  ➔   H - C - H
         •              |
         H              H
   ```

## 2. Allotropes of Carbon

| Allotrope | Structural Characteristics | Physical Properties & Uses |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Diamond** | Each carbon atom is bonded to $4$ other carbon atoms, forming a **rigid 3D structure**. | * Extremely hard (hardest known natural substance).<br>* Electrical insulator (no free electrons). |
| **Graphite** | Each carbon atom is bonded to $3$ other carbon atoms in the same plane, forming **hexagonal layers**. One of the bonds is a double bond. | * Soft and slippery.<br>* Good conductor of electricity due to free valence electrons. |
| **Buckminsterfullerene (C₆₀)** | Carbon atoms are arranged in the **shape of a football**. | * Used in nanotechnology and specialized chemical research. |

## 3. Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon forms a massive number of compounds due to two unique properties of carbon atoms:
1. **Catenation:** Carbon has the unique ability to form bonds with other atoms of carbon, giving rise to large molecules. These can form straight chains, branched chains, or rings (cyclic structures).
2. **Tetravalency:** Since carbon has a valency of $4$, it is capable of bonding with four other atoms of carbon or atoms of some other monovalent elements (like oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, sulfur, and chlorine).

### 4.2 Structural Isomerism
Compounds with identical molecular formulae but different structural formulae are called **structural isomers**.
* *Example:* Isomers of Butane ($\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$):

1. **n-Butane (Straight chain):**
   ```text
     H   H   H   H
     |   |   |   |
   H-C - C - C - C-H
     |   |   |   |
     H   H   H   H
   ```
2. **Isobutane (2-Methylpropane - Branched chain):**
   ```text
     H   H   H
     |   |   |
   H-C - C - C-H
     |   |   |
     H   |   H
       H-C-H
         |
         H
   ```

## 6. Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

### 6.1 Combustion
* **Saturated hydrocarbons:** Burn with a clean blue flame.
* **Unsaturated hydrocarbons:** Burn with a yellow flame releasing lots of black soot (sooty flame).
  $$2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} + 6\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 4\text{CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{Heat} + \text{Light}$$

> [!NOTE]
> **Formation of Coal and Petroleum:**
> Coal and petroleum were formed from ancient organic matter (trees, ferns, and marine plants/animals) buried under the earth millions of years ago, exposed to intense heat and pressure. Coal is formed from plant remains under geological compression, while petroleum is formed from the decomposition of microscopic marine organisms.

### 6.2 Oxidation
Alcohols can be oxidized to carboxylic acids using oxidizing agents:
$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH (Ethanol)} \xrightarrow{\text{Alkaline } \text{KMnO}_4 + \Delta \text{ or } \text{Acidified } \text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7 + \Delta} \text{CH}_3\text{COOH (Ethanoic Acid)}$$

### 6.3 Addition Reaction
Unsaturated hydrocarbons add hydrogen in the presence of metal catalysts to form saturated hydrocarbons:
$$\text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2\text{ (Ethene)} + \text{H}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Ni / Pd}} \text{CH}_3-\text{CH}_3\text{ (Ethane)}$$
* **Hydrogenation of Vegetable Oils:** Vegetable oils (unsaturated fats) are liquid at room temp and are healthy, while animal fats (saturated fats) are solid and unhealthy.

### 6.4 Substitution Reaction
Saturated hydrocarbons react with Chlorine in the presence of sunlight:
$$\text{CH}_4 + \text{Cl}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Sunlight}} \text{CH}_3\text{Cl} + \text{HCl}$$

## 7. Important Carbon Compounds: Ethanol & Ethanoic Acid

### 7.1 Ethanol ($\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH}$)
* **Reaction with Sodium (Hydrogen Gas evolution):**
  $$2\text{Na} + 2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \rightarrow 2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{O}^-\text{Na}^+\text{ (Sodium Ethoxide)} + \text{H}_2(g)$$
* **Dehydration:** Heating ethanol with concentrated sulfuric acid converts it to ethene:
  $$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{Conc. } \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4, 443\text{K}} \text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
  *(Conc. $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ acts as a dehydrating agent)*

### 7.2 Ethanoic Acid ($\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$)
Commonly called **Acetic Acid**. A $5-8\%$ solution of acetic acid in water is called **Vinegar**. Pure ethanoic acid has a melting point of $290\text{ K}$ ($17^\circ\text{C}$) and freezes in winter, hence it is also called **Glacial Acetic Acid**.

* **Esterification:**
  $$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{Acid Catalyst}} \text{CH}_3\text{COOCH}_2\text{CH}_3\text{ (Ester)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
* **Saponification (Soap formation):**
  $$\text{CH}_3\text{COOCH}_2\text{CH}_3 + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa (Soap)} + \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH}$$
* **Neutralization with Base:**
  $$\text{NaOH} + \text{CH}_3\text{COOH} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
* **Reaction with Carbonate / Hydrogen Carbonate:**
  $$2\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow 2\text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2$$
  $$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{NaHCO}_3 \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2$$

## 8. Soaps and Detergents

### 8.1 Structure of Soap and Micelle Formation
A soap molecule is a sodium or potassium salt of a long-chain carboxylic acid.

```text
       (Hydrophobic hydrocarbon tail)          (Hydrophilic ionic head)
      \/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\ (COO⁻ Na⁺)
      (Grease-loving)                              (Water-loving)
```

In water, soap molecules align in a cluster called a **Micelle**:
The hydrophobic tails dissolve in the grease/dirt droplet, while the hydrophilic heads face outward in the water, suspending the dirt in an emulsion to be washed away.

### 8.2 Comparison: Soap vs Detergent

| Property | Soap | Detergent |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chemical Structure** | Sodium/potassium salts of long-chain fatty acids. | Ammonium or sulfonate salts of long-chain carboxylic acids. |
| **Action in Hard Water** | Reacts with $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ ions to form an insoluble curdy precipitate called **Scum**. Does not lather well. | Does not form curdy precipitate with calcium/magnesium ions. **Lathers easily even in hard water**. |
| **Cleaning Suitability** | Suitable for cleaning in soft water only. | Highly effective cleaning in both soft and hard water. |
