## 1. Bonding in Carbon - The Covalent Bond

The chemical reactivity of elements is driven by their tendency to attain a stable, noble gas configuration. 

- Carbon has an atomic number of $6$, with an electronic configuration of **$2, 4$**. 
- It has $4$ valence electrons in its outermost shell.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why Carbon does not form Ionic Bonds?**
> * **Inability to form $C^{4-}$ Anion:** If carbon gains $4$ electrons, it would be extremely difficult for the nucleus containing $6$ protons to hold onto $10$ electrons (i.e., $4$ extra electrons).
> * **Inability to form $C^{4+}$ Cation:** If carbon loses $4$ electrons, removing $4$ electrons requires a massive amount of energy, leaving a carbon cation with only $2$ electrons held by $6$ protons.
> * **Conclusion:** Carbon overcomes this barrier by sharing its valence electrons with other atoms of carbon or other elements to form **Covalent Bonds**.

### 1.1 General Properties of Covalent Compounds
* **Melting and Boiling Points:** The intermolecular forces between covalent molecules are very weak. Consequently, covalent compounds have **low melting and boiling points**.
* **Electrical Conductivity:** Since electrons are shared and no charged particles (ions) are formed, covalent compounds are generally **poor/non-conductors of electricity**.

## 2. Allotropes of Carbon

Carbon occurs in nature in several allotropic forms with widely different physical properties.

| Allotrope | Structural Characteristics | Physical Properties & Uses |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Diamond** | Each carbon atom is bonded to $4$ other carbon atoms, forming a **rigid 3D structure**. | * Extremely hard (hardest known natural substance).<br>* Electrical insulator (no free electrons). |
| **Graphite** | Each carbon atom is bonded to $3$ other carbon atoms in the same plane, forming **hexagonal layers**. One of the bonds is a double bond. | * Soft and slippery.<br>* Good conductor of electricity due to free valence electrons. |
| **Buckminsterfullerene (C₆₀)** | Carbon atoms are arranged in the **shape of a football**. | * Used in nanotechnology and specialized chemical research. |

## 3. Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon forms a massive number of compounds due to three unique properties:
1. **Catenation:** Carbon has the unique ability to form covalent bonds with other atoms of carbon, giving rise to long chains, branched chains, or cyclic rings.
2. **Tetravalency:** Since carbon has a valency of $4$, it is capable of bonding with four other atoms of carbon or atoms of some other monovalent elements (like oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, sulfur, and chlorine).
3. **Small Atomic Size of Carbon:** 
   > [!TIP]
   > The bond formed by carbon is exceptionally strong because of its small size. This allows the nucleus to hold the shared pairs of electrons strongly, making carbon compounds **extremely stable**.

## 4. Saturated and Unsaturated Carbon Compounds

Compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen are called **Hydrocarbons**. They are divided into two categories:

1. **Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes):**
   * Compounds where the carbon atoms are linked by **single bonds** only.
   * These are generally not very reactive.
   * **General Formula:** $C_nH_{2n+2}$
2. **Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:**
   * Compounds where carbon atoms are linked by **double or triple bonds**.
   * Compounds with one or more double bonds are called **Alkenes**. (General Formula: $C_nH_{2n}$)
   * Compounds with one or more triple bonds are called **Alkynes**. (General Formula: $C_nH_{2n-2}$)
   * These are **highly reactive** compared to saturated hydrocarbons.

## 6. Heteroatoms, Functional Groups & Homologous Series

### 6.1 Heteroatoms & Functional Groups
* **Heteroatom (Odd Element):** In a hydrocarbon chain, elements replacing one or more hydrogen atoms (such as halogens $\text{Cl/Br}$, oxygen, nitrogen, and sulfur) are called heteroatoms.
* **Functional Group:** A group of heteroatoms that confers specific chemical properties to organic compounds, independent of the length and nature of the carbon chain.

#### Table 4.3: Functional Groups in Carbon Compounds

| Heteroatom | Functional Group | Formula |
| :--- | :--- | :---: |
| **Halogen (Cl / Br)** | Halo (Chloro / Bromo) | $-\text{Cl}$, $-\text{Br}$ |
| **Oxygen** | Alcohol | $-\text{OH}$ |
| | Aldehyde | $-\text{CHO}$ |
| | Ketone | $>\text{C}=\text{O}$ |
| | Carboxylic acid | $-\text{COOH}$ |

### 6.2 Homologous Series
A series of carbon compounds in which the same functional group substitutes for hydrogen in a carbon chain.
* Consecutive members differ by a **$-\text{CH}_2-$** unit.
* Consecutive members differ by **$14 \text{ u}$** in molecular mass.
* **General Formulas:**
  * Alkanes: $C_nH_{2n+2}$
  * Alkenes: $C_nH_{2n}$
  * Alkynes: $C_nH_{2n-2}$

## 7. Chemical Properties of Carbon Compounds

### 7.1 Combustion
* **Saturated Hydrocarbons:** Burn with a clean blue flame in sufficient oxygen.
* **Incomplete Combustion:** In a limited supply of air, saturated hydrocarbons burn with a yellow sooty flame due to unburnt carbon.
* **Unsaturated Hydrocarbons:** Always burn with a yellow flame releasing black soot.
  $$2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} + 6\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 4\text{CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{Heat} + \text{Light}$$

> [!NOTE]
> **Formation of Coal and Petroleum:**
> * **Coal:** Formed from compressed remains of ancient forests and land plants buried under tectonic geological shifts millions of years ago. Under high heat and pressure, it converted to coal.
> * **Petroleum:** Formed from compressed remains of tiny marine organisms. When they died, their bodies settled at the sea bed and were covered with layers of silt/sand. Under millions of years of high heat, pressure, and anaerobic conditions, they decayed into petroleum oil and natural gas.

### 7.2 Oxidation
* **Oxidizing Agents:** Substances that are capable of adding oxygen to other substances (e.g., Alkaline Potassium Permanganate $\text{KMnO}_4$ and Acidified Potassium Dichromate $\text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7$).
  $$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH (Ethanol)} \xrightarrow{\text{Alkaline } \text{KMnO}_4 + \Delta \text{ or } \text{Acidified } \text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7 + \Delta} \text{CH}_3\text{COOH (Ethanoic Acid)}$$

### 7.3 Addition Reaction
* **Catalyst:** Substances that cause a reaction to occur or proceed at a different rate without themselves being affected or consumed (e.g., Nickel $\text{Ni}$ or Palladium $\text{Pd}$).
* Unsaturated hydrocarbons add hydrogen in the presence of catalysts to form saturated hydrocarbons:
  $$\text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2\text{ (Ethene)} + \text{H}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Ni / Pd}} \text{CH}_3-\text{CH}_3\text{ (Ethane)}$$
* **Hydrogenation of Vegetable Oils:** Vegetable oils (unsaturated fats) have long unsaturated carbon chains and are healthy, whereas animal fats (saturated fats) are unhealthy and are linked to cardiovascular diseases.

### 7.4 Substitution Reaction
Saturated hydrocarbons react with Chlorine in the presence of sunlight in a rapid reaction where chlorine replaces hydrogen atoms one by one:
$$\text{CH}_4 + \text{Cl}_2 \xrightarrow{\text{Sunlight}} \text{CH}_3\text{Cl} + \text{HCl}$$

## 8. Important Carbon Compounds: Ethanol & Ethanoic Acid

### 8.1 Ethanol ($\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH}$)
* **Physical Properties:** Melting point: **$156\text{ K}$**, Boiling point: **$351\text{ K}$**.
* **Solubility & Solvent Properties:** It is an excellent solvent, hence used in medicines like **Tincture of Iodine, cough syrups, and tonics**.
* **Health Effects:** Ethanol consumption causes intoxication. Consuming pure ethanol (absolute alcohol) can be **fatal**.

> [!WARNING]
> **Methanol Toxicity:**
> Methanol ($\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$) is highly toxic. In the liver, it is oxidized to **Methanal** ($\text{HCHO}$), which coagulates cellular protoplasm (similar to boiling an egg). Methanal also damages the optic nerve, causing permanent blindness or death.
> 
> **Denatured Alcohol:**
> Industrial ethanol is mixed with toxic substances like methanol, pyridine, or blue copper sulphate dyes to prevent its misuse as a beverage. This is called denatured alcohol.

> [!NOTE]
> **Alcohol as a Fuel:**
> Sugar cane juice is fermented to produce molasses, which is then distilled to obtain ethanol. It is mixed with petrol (gasohol) as a clean-burning fuel additive, emitting only $\text{CO}_2$ and water upon combustion.

* **Reaction with Sodium:**
  $$2\text{Na} + 2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \rightarrow 2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{O}^-\text{Na}^+\text{ (Sodium Ethoxide)} + \text{H}_2(g)$$
* **Dehydration:** Heating ethanol with concentrated sulfuric acid converts it to ethene:
  $$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{Conc. } \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4, 443\text{K}} \text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
  *(Conc. $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ acts as a dehydrating agent)*

### 8.2 Ethanoic Acid / Acetic Acid ($\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$)
* **Vinegar:** A $3-4\%$ (or $5-8\%$) solution of acetic acid in water is called vinegar, used as a preservative in pickles.
* **Glacial Acetic Acid:** Pure ethanoic acid has a melting point of **$290\text{ K}$ ($17^\circ\text{C}$)**. In cold climates, it freezes into ice-like crystals, hence the name glacial acetic acid.
* **Reactions:**
  * **Esterification:** Acid + Alcohol $\rightarrow$ Sweet-smelling Ester:
    $$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{Acid Catalyst}} \text{CH}_3\text{COOCH}_2\text{CH}_3\text{ (Ester)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}$$
  * **Saponification:** Reaction of Ester with NaOH to yield soap and alcohol:
    $$\text{CH}_3\text{COOCH}_2\text{CH}_3 + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa (Soap)} + \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH}$$
  * **Reaction with Carbonate / Hydrogen Carbonate:**
    $$2\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{Na}_2\text{CO}_3 \rightarrow 2\text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2$$
    $$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{NaHCO}_3 \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa} + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2$$

## 9. Soaps and Detergents

### 9.1 Structure of Soap and Micelle Formation
Soap molecules consist of a long hydrocarbon tail and a sodium/potassium carboxylate head.
1. **Hydrophilic Head (Water-loving):** The ionic head ($\text{COO}^-\text{Na}^+$) that dissolves in water.
2. **Hydrophobic Tail (Grease-loving):** The long hydrocarbon tail that dissolves in oils and grease.

* **Micelle:** In water, soap molecules cluster in a spherical shape. The hydrophobic tails point inward, capturing oil/dirt droplets in the center, while the hydrophilic heads face outward, forming a stable emulsion in water.

### 9.2 Action in Hard Water (Soft Water vs Hard Water)
* **Soft Water:** Lathers easily with soap.
* **Hard Water:** Water containing calcium ($\text{Ca}^{2+}$) and magnesium ($\text{Mg}^{2+}$) salts (hydrogencarbonates, sulphates, or chlorides).

> [!WARNING]
> **Scum Formation:**
> Soap reacts with the calcium and magnesium ions in hard water to form an insoluble curdy precipitate called **Scum**, which wastes soap and sticks to surfaces.
> 
> **Detergents** solve this problem. Detergents are ammonium or sulfonate salts of long-chain carboxylic acids. Their charged ends do not form insoluble precipitates with calcium/magnesium ions in hard water, allowing them to remain highly effective cleaners in both soft and hard water.

### 9.3 Comparison: Soap vs Detergent

| Property | Soap | Detergent |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chemical Structure** | Sodium or potassium salts of long-chain carboxylic (fatty) acids. | Ammonium or sulfonate salts of long-chain carboxylic acids. |
| **Action in Hard Water** | Reacts with $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ and $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ ions to form an insoluble curdy precipitate called **Scum**. Does not lather well. | Does not form insoluble precipitates with calcium or magnesium ions. **Lathers easily even in hard water**. |
| **Cleaning Suitability** | Suitable for cleaning in soft water only. | Highly effective cleaning in both soft and hard water. |
| **Common Uses** | Primarily used for bathing and handwashing soaps. | Primarily used in shampoos and laundry detergents (washing powders/liquids). |
